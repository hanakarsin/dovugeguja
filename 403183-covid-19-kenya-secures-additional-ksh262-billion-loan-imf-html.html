COVID-19: Kenya secures additional KSh 262 billion loan from IMF

Posted by Almeda Bohannan on Monday, July 8, 2024

- The money will be disbursed in the coming months following an agreement and will run for a consecutive three years

- The loan is the second for Kenya after another KSh 73 billion was secured on May 2020

- As hinted by the treasury, the latest monies will be used to cover the economic gaps occasioned by the COVID-19 ravages

Kenya now awaits another KSh 262 billion loan from the International Monetary Fund (IMF) after the agency approved the request which was fronted late 2020.

According to the IMF, the credit will be on a 38-month basis with disbursements expected to begin in a few months to come.

The funds will be channeled to reviving the economy after the country exhausted its reserves in responding to the pandemic which was first reported on March 13, 2020.

"I am pleased to announce that the Kenyan authorities and the IMF mission team have reached agreement on economic and structural policies that would underpin a 38-month program under the EFF and ECF arrangements for about US$2.4 billion,” said IMF's Mary Goodman."The staff-level agreement is subject to IMF management approval and Executive Board consideration, which is expected in the coming weeks," Goodman added.

Concerns on loan usage

During the time of the loan request in December last year, TUKO.co.ke reported that no specific project had been tied to the loan.

There were concerns that the loan would be misused in funding political activities such as the BBI once it is approved.

The air has, however, been cleared,with the IMF saying the money is aimed at funding development projects as well as covering the budgetary deficits following the disruption by COVID-19.

The pandemic had a negative impact on the country's revenue collection as the economy got limping following the unfriendly anti-corona protocols that were being effected.

As observed by Business Daily, revenue collection in the first six months to December experienced a deficit of KSh 107 billion away from the targeted KSh 907.7 billion, this underscoring the challenges of the pandemic’s disruptions on businesses.

The KSh 262 billion loan adds to the bucket of other loans and grants Kenya has secured since the COVID-19 began plaguing the country.

On March 16, 2020, just three days after recording the first case of the virus, the country received KSh 6.1 billion from the World Bank to help combat the infection.

Also on May 21, 2020, the World Bank in a statement disclosed it had approved another KSh 4.59 billion to back Kenya's fight against desert locusts.

A day earlier, the same bank had approved a loan of KSh 106.8 billion to close the fiscal financing gap generated by the impact of COVID-19 on the country's economy.
