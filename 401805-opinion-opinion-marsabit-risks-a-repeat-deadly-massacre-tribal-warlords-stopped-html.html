Opinion: Marsabit risks a repeat of deadly massacre if tribal warlords are not stopped

Posted by Lourie Helzer on Wednesday, August 21, 2024

The ongoing fighting in Marsabit county is a very unfortunate affair that could have a disastrous ending if not urgently addressed.

Several people have lost their lives and many others displaced as a result of politically-motivated inter-clan clashes in the county.

Instead of taking advantage of devolution to propel the previously marginalised area to greater heights, local politicians have resorted to fanning and financing violence in pursuit of their own selfish interests.

The government must put its foot down to ensure that the culprits, the perpetrators and the sponsors of the clashes, are brought to book and ensure residents live peacefully.

Historically, the fighting in Marsabit, which often pits the Borana against the Gabra in Turbi area, has been caused by the scarcity of water and pasture.

It is an unhealthy competition that has resulted in massive loss of lives and wanton destruction of property.

Efforts by the government to restore peace and foster unity among locals have previously hit a snag.

In one of the darkest episodes, 70 people, 22 of them being children, lost their lives in July 2005 in what is known as the Turbi massacre.

Over 6,000 people were evicted from their homes and sought refuge in Marsabit town.

In a bid to reach a peaceful settlement, a high-powered delegation set out for the region on April 10, 2006.

It consisted of the region's favourite sons, North Horr MP and former Foreign Affairs minister Dr Bonaya Godana, Laisamis MP and Regional Development assistant minister Titus Ngoyoni, Saku MP Abdi Sasura, Moyale MP Guracha Galgallo and EALA MP Lt. Gen. Abdullahi Adan.

Also in the team was Internal Security assistant minister Mirugi Kariuki and Moyale district commissioner Peter King’ola.

However, the mission turned tragic when they all perished after the Kenya Air Force plane they were travelling in from Nairobi crashed near Marsabit town. 14 people died in total, including members of the clergy.

Earlier in July 1996, Provincial Commissioner Ishmael Juma Chelang’a also perished while on a similar peace mission to the area.

It is clear that local leaders never learnt from this sad history as they continue to foment animosity between the two clans with disastrous consequences.

The current infighting has its roots in these historical feuds, it has now taken a new shape as a result of toxic supremacy battles among scheming politicians as noted by Interior Cabinet Secretary DR Fred Matiang’i.

There are reports that Governor Mohammed Mohamud Ali and Isiolo County Women rep Rehema Dida Jaldesa are behind the ongoing fighting that has resulted in avoidable loss of lives, displacement of local populations, malicious destruction of property, and disruption of economic activities in the county.

This is despite Jaldesa being from a different county. It also appears the governor views himself as a Borana governor and not the governor of Marsabit, which, incidentally, apart from the two feuding clans, is also home to the Burji, Rendille, Sakuye, Turkana and Gurreh, who also need to live in peace in their own county.

According to Dr Matiang’i, competition for the control of power and county resources has intensified since the onset of devolution in 2013.

There are even claims that some county officials fuel acts of violence as a way of escaping accountability over the use of devolved funds.

It is clear that county funds are being misdirected to fund the clan wars in order to protect the political interests of a few.

In the current conflict, two Marsabit County government employees were among four people arrested by the Border Patrol Unit (BPU) during an operation in Turbi area. During the arrest, police said they recovered a rifle and 10 bullets.

Uran ward member of County Assembly, Halkano Konso, has since surrendered to the police over his alleged role in the conflict.

If true, such a situation cannot be condoned as it is the ordinary citizens who suffer double jeopardy; they bear the brunt of the violence and are also denied development and the public services they are entitled to as a right.

It is time the people of Marsabit realise that they are being played against each other by cunning politicians and embrace peace and harmony if they are to realise greater development. It is likely that as the 2022 elections near, the politicians will escalate the hostility for their selfish gain. The people must say ‘No’ to this kind of politics.

If the politicians are interested in sustaining the conflict, they should take their kids who are comfortably living, working or schooling in Nairobi to the frontline.

On the other hand, the authorities need to ensure that all politicians involved in the violence are arrested without discrimination if a lasting solution is to be found.

The Marsabit leaders also need to be reminded that the Marsabit conflict is a local problem that needs local solutions and they should thus stop escalating it into a national agenda.

While the government has an obligation to ensure peace across the territory of Kenya, it is the Marsabit leaders who hold the key to sorting out this mess.

They need to be more responsible with their words and actions and realise that Marsabit is a heterogeneous county with great economic potential and the residents must thus be encouraged to co-exist peacefully.
